The domain represents the operation and organization of a train system, focusing on its structural components and the rules governing their interactions. A train, identified uniquely, consists of a series of interconnected wagons, each contributing to the train's weight and functional load. Wagons, capable of transporting goods or passengers, have defined capacities and smoking allowances, and their load must not exceed their designed capacity, ensuring operational safety and efficiency. The system enforces consistency by requiring all trains to have the same number of wagons, reflecting standardized configurations across the fleet. The sequential arrangement of wagons is captured through successor and predecessor relationships, ensuring a logical flow and maintaining that each wagon pair belongs to the same train. Additionally, constraints on train length, which includes wagons and engines, prevent excessive size, optimizing maneuverability and infrastructure compatibility. Importantly, every train is required to have at least one engine, underscoring the operational necessity of propulsion. Collectively, these structural definitions and constraints reflect a well-organized system prioritizing reliability, safety, and consistency in train operations, aligning with real-world logistical and operational demands.
